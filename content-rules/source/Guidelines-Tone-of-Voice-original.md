## **Audience: Who are we speaking to?**

How do we connect with the 65-year-old rocker and the 21-year-old pianist simultaneously? We split them by their ambitions and how much they play.

| **Weekend Warriors** | The type of musician you might consider semi-professional. They're not professional in that they are not making a living performing music; they have a regular job, usually outside of music. In their free time, they perform whenever and wherever they can. While they'll sometimes get paid for performances, that money is often going back into buying better gear or something else related to making music.
This is your local bar band, found in every city worldwide, playing covers and sometimes original songs, living their rock-n-roll dream. They're generally older (in the late 30s to 60s) and no longer dream of making it big, but they still love to play. |
| --- | --- |
| **Hobbyists** | This is your typical bedroom musician. Someone who casually enjoys creating music — most often, they will be making music at home (especially using home studio software and gear) but will occasionally perform (probably not for any or much money).
 |
| **Pros** | These are professionals; who'd have thought? Our focus isn't as much on these people right now; this could change in the future. So let's concentrate on those people who want a little more creativity in their everyday lives, have something to say with music, and just want to make a noise on a Saturday night. |

## **Extra tips**

**Keep it current**

- Stay up-to-date with current musicians as well as the classics
- Look at the current top tabs and scores in-app to see what's popular.

**Keep it inclusive**

- Use a mix of genders in your examples and don't use outdated views on gender
- Showcase female, trans, or non-binary musicians in the community
- Encourage and reward the small stuff
- Don't patronize or talk down to beginners.
- Don't only focus on Rock

# **Key rules to make it Muse**

### **Top 5 rules**

| **One: Make it clear** | Does it tell the user what you need it to do in the most simple way? |
| --- | --- |
| **Two: Create an emotional connection** | Your writing should connect with people on an emotional level, it should inspire them to **unleash their sound**. This can be any emotion; you can make your reader laugh, be inspired, or be ready to take their anger out on their guitar, drums, or ukelele. |
| **Three: Do a grammar and spelling check** | Make sure you've run a check, whether with [Grammarly](https://app.grammarly.com/) or the spellcheck on the software you're using. Ensure your product names and features are correctly formatted in US English, and that your use of tense and capital letters is consistent. **All the little details matter**. |
| **Four: Add some rock 'n' roll in there** | This is our way of saying, 'Is it on-brand?'. We want our copy to be creative, but never at the expense of clarity. You may not have space for rock 'n' roll on an in-app button, but maybe you can inject some into that CRM brief or banner headline. Is there something you can add to your copy to **make it Muse?** Striking the balance and knowing when to use it is key.
**Important note. Rock 'n' roll doesn't need to mean rock music, it's the character of the brand, the spirit, the music.* |
| **The Final Rule: Turn it to eleven** 
 | This is where we turn **good copy into great copy**. Take one last look at your copy, and see if there is anything that can be given a twist or tweak to give it that special something. For instance, could a cliche be played with? Is your example the best one? Have you used Slayer too many times?
 |

# Things to avoid

Every product we create is for everyone. Nobody should feel excluded or uncomfortable due to our tone. We know that swearing is permitted on some brands, as is a little edge. But it's a fine line to tread, if in doubt sound it out to the wider team and get their thoughts.

| **Edgy vs Edgelord** | As a brand Muse Group is known for being brave, edgy, relatable, and inclusive. Our products are for everyone, which means Muse has a responsibility to define its level of edge. We want to be a cool brand, a brand for everyone, not a try-hard cringe one that might offend people with risky content. This is NOT censorship. It is finding a way to retain our edge while being inclusive to everyone. |
| --- | --- |
| **Definitions** | Edgy: Edgy is when someone/something is being real, being themselves. Being honest in a provocative, not dodgy way. 

Edgelord*: Someone who's online too much and trying to get attention by posting shocking and nihilistic words and opinions that they may or may not believe. AVOID AVOID (p.s. Gen Z and Gen Alpha hate them). |
| **Why do we need a little edge?** | As a brand is a visionary rebel, we're provocative, unapologetically ourselves, and not afraid to stand out and make some noise. We became popular because people love our products and our ethos of supporting musicians to express themselves and share their wildest ideas. Being different and being bold is important to who we were when we started and who we continue to be. |

# **Grammar**

Let's take a look at grammar rules for Muse.

### **Simple rules**

|  | **Rule** |
| --- | --- |
| Full stops/period (.)
 | Don’t put full stops in headlines or in description copy on the site. Everywhere else, use as standard. |
| Quotation marks | We never put quotation marks around the names of our products or any of our features. However, do use them for actual quotes in editorial work. |
| Exclamation marks!!! | If something is super exciting, an exclamation mark is a good choice; just keep it to a minimum. As a general rule, 2 is generally 2 many!! |
| Hyphenation
 | Hyphens help clarify our message when we use compound words. |
| Colon | We use colons to draw attention to important information by inserting a colon before a list of bullet points. |
| The Oxford comma
 | We use the Oxford comma when writing a list of items. We use it after the second to last item in the list, before the final "and" or "or." |
| Naming | Use capital letters for proper nouns. We use them for products, brands, promotions, company names and features. As well as people, places and institutions. |
| Song, playlist and course names | Use title case to capitalize first letter of every word. |
| UX titles &
headlines, Banners, Body copy and Buttons | Use sentence case. Only capitalize the first letter of the first word and all proper nouns. One exception is the word after a number, e.g., 90% 0ff should be 90% off. |
| Music genres | Most music genres are not proper nouns, and therefore should notbe capitalized. |

# Accessibility

Music is for everyone! How Muse creates accessible content

We want to reach as wide an audience as possible and ensure everyone can access our content and music-making tools. Well-written copy that is easy to understand makes life easier for everyone. Therefore, it should always be a consideration when writing any copy.

Copywriters should use the [**Accessibility Checklist**](https://ds-foundation.mu.se/accessibility-strategy) to determine whether their copy is accessible.
